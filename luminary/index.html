The University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill (UNC-CH) was built over 200 years with little to no consideration for mobility challenges. In recent years, the university has made efforts to address some of its accessibility issues such as the modernization of some elevators and the construction of a permanent ramp on the Old Well to allow students with limited mobility to participate in the first day of class tradition to sip from the fountain. However, navigating the campus remains difficult, especially for those with disabilities. To name just a couple daily obstacles; there is constant construction blocking pathways, and the brick sidewalks are uneven and often missing bricks, leading to large, deep puddles when it rains. These are the kinds of barriers that highlight the need for a tool to make navigating campus easier, which is where Luminary comes in.

In early 2023, Tar Heels at the Table (THATT), an accessibility advocacy group on campus, reached out to App Team Carolina, a nonprofit organization on campus that builds iOS apps, with the idea to make an app that informs users about temporary and permanent accessibility obstacles on campus and provides alternate routes to locations based on the user‚Äôs unique accessibility preferences. As a founding executive member of App Team Carolina, I was super eager to take on the project not only a UI/UX designer, but also as a team lead for the project. This means that in addition to creating the designs for the app, I also guide a dynamic team of developers, designers, marketers, and product managers toward release to the App Store.

This project is particularly dear to me as it aligns perfectly with my passion for creating inclusive designs that work for everyone, regardless of ability. Luminary has also been my first opportunity to shepherd a project from initial concept through to completion, allowing me to apply my design philosophy in a meaningful way that directly impacts my campus community.

Preliminary Research

After establishing the app‚Äôs concept and purpose with THATT, the first step for our team was to get a better understanding of the specific types of issues students face on campus in terms of accessibility. In order to do this, I worked with our product managers to create a survey asking respondents to select issues from a checklist (or write in new ones) that are prominent in their experience navigating campus and share other details about their experiences. This survey was distributed to students with disabilities as well as accessibility advocates through THATT and UNC‚Äôs Equal Opportunity and Compliance office.

Defining Features

At this point, we defined the features to include in Luminary and ranked them by priority. The four features listed below were deemed necessary for our minimum viable product because they provide the most complete solution to the problem at hand while still being technically feasible in a reasonable amount of time.

Obstacle reporting
Outdoor turn-by-turn navigation
Obstacle avoidance preferences
Accessible entrace locating



The next three features were deemed unnecessary for the minimum viable product but still feasible and valuable enough to be included in the next version of Luminary.

Obstacle notifications
Accessible restroom locating
User interaction (i.e. commenting, upvoting)



These last two features are our "moonshot" features, which would be very valuable to Luminary's users, but they are not currently feasible with our development and budgetary restraints.

Indoor turn-by-turn navigation
Noise/crowd level indicators

Branding

Prior to converting the wireframe into a high-fidelity prototype, it was important to establish the visual identity of the app. When the project was brought to App Team Carolina, it was called Polaris. After some research on existing apps, products, and other search results online and on the App Store, I decided to rebrand the app to ensure a more distinct and memorable identity.

For the name, I searched for synonyms of words related to wayfinding and came up with the following options, of which the team and I chose Luminary as the best option.

Current Version

By August 2024, I had completed two summer internships as a product designer since making the first version above, and I knew there was a lot of room for improvement. I decided to revamp the design with the main goals of making it cleaner, more intuitive for users, and more interactive in the prototype.

The biggest updates made for this new version were:

  • Removing the tab bar because I felt navigating the app was aimpler without it.

  • Adding an onboarding sequence to collect the obstacles the user needs to avoid in routing and add favorites before entering the app.

  • Adding a new feature called quick reports, which allows users to report common obstacles without having to fill out the form every time.
